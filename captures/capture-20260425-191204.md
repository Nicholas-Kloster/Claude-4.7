%%FILL: the Kaplan vs. Chinchilla scaling laws — what changed and why it mattered%%

Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020) found loss scaled as a power law in compute, parameters, and data — and that for a fixed compute budget you should scale parameters much faster than data. That recipe drove GPT-3 (175B params, ~300B tokens — wildly under-trained by what came next).

Hoffmann et al. (DeepMind, 2022, "Chinchilla") re-ran the sweep with better methodology: held compute fixed, varied the param/token split, fit a parametric loss surface. Result: compute-optimal training wants parameters and tokens to scale roughly 1:1, not 1:0.3. Chinchilla (70B, 1.4T tokens) beat Gopher (280B, 300B tokens) at 4× less compute.

Why it mattered: Kaplan's law made the field over-parameterize and under-train. Most pre-Chinchilla flagship models were leaving capability on the table — you got more out of feeding a smaller model more tokens than scaling the model. The cause of the discrepancy was largely Kaplan using a fixed LR schedule that didn't decay properly for longer runs; the optimization was leaving signal on the floor at high token counts, biasing the fit toward "params matter more." Chinchilla also reframed the economics — inference cost is dominated by params, so a smaller-but-better-trained model is cheaper to *serve*, not just to train. Llama, Llama 2, Mistral, and the entire open-weights wave that followed are downstream of this correction.

%%FILL: the precise functional form of the scaling law and what each variable is%%

Chinchilla's parametric form for cross-entropy loss as a function of parameters and tokens:

```
L(N, D) = E + A/N^α + B/D^β
```

- `L` — cross-entropy loss in nats per token on the held-out distribution
- `N` — non-embedding parameter count
- `D` — training tokens
- `E` — irreducible loss / entropy of the data distribution (the floor; you can't get below this no matter how big the model)
- `A`, `B` — fitted constants
- `α`, `β` — fitted exponents controlling how fast loss falls with N and D

Hoffmann et al.'s fit: `E ≈ 1.69`, `A ≈ 406.4`, `B ≈ 410.7`, `α ≈ 0.34`, `β ≈ 0.28`.

Compute constraint (FLOPs): `C ≈ 6 N D` for dense transformers (Kaplan's approximation — 2 for the forward, 4 for the backward, per param per token). Minimizing `L` subject to fixed `C` via Lagrange multipliers gives the compute-optimal allocation:

```
N_opt ∝ C^a,  D_opt ∝ C^b,  where a = β/(α+β), b = α/(α+β)
```

With Chinchilla's exponents, `a ≈ 0.45`, `b ≈ 0.55` — close to the empirical "~20 tokens per parameter" rule of thumb.

Kaplan's earlier form was similar in shape (separate power laws in N, D, C) but with very different exponents — his params-dominant fit gave the bad allocation.

%%FILL: what scaling laws assume about the data distribution and when those assumptions break%%

Core assumptions, mostly unstated:

1. **IID samples from a fixed distribution.** The fit assumes each token is drawn from the same underlying process. Breaks immediately when training data is curated, deduplicated, filtered by quality classifiers, or upsampled (code, math, instruction data). The "effective" D is no longer the raw token count.
2. **Single-epoch training.** Chinchilla fit on data seen once. Repeating tokens degrades the per-token value — Muennighoff et al. (2023, "Scaling Data-Constrained LMs") showed up to ~4 epochs is roughly free, then returns collapse. Once you're in the data-constrained regime (which frontier labs hit around 2023–2024 for high-quality web text), the law's `D` axis stops being linear.
3. **Stationary distribution.** No distribution shift between train and eval. Breaks for any deployed model — the internet at inference time is not the internet at training time, and increasingly contains model output (training on synthetic / model-generated text changes the curve, often badly — see "model collapse" papers, Shumailov et al. 2023).
4. **Loss is the right objective.** The law predicts cross-entropy on web text. It says nothing about RLHF, instruction-following, tool use, or any post-training objective. Once you fine-tune, the pretraining scaling law is no longer governing the metric you care about.
5. **Architecture is fixed.** Exponents are specific to dense decoder-only transformers with the tokenizer and context length used. MoE breaks `C ≈ 6ND` (active vs. total params). Long-context, sparse attention, and state-space models all need their own fits.
6. **Quality is constant across the dataset.** A token of Wikipedia is treated the same as a token of SEO spam. Quality filtering empirically buys ~2–3× effective compute at fixed nominal D, which the raw law can't see.

When the assumption stack holds — fresh, IID, single-epoch web text on a vanilla dense transformer — the law is remarkably tight. Outside that, it's a guideline, not a prediction.

%%FILL: a capability that emerged at a specific scale that scaling laws didn't predict%%

In-context learning at GPT-3 scale (~13B–175B) is the canonical example, but the cleaner case is **chain-of-thought reasoning on multi-step arithmetic and word problems**.

Wei et al. (2022, "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning") showed that on GSM8K and similar benchmarks, performance was at chance / near-flat across model sizes from ~1B to ~62B params — and then jumped sharply between 62B and 540B (PaLM). Below the threshold, prompting "let's think step by step" did nothing or hurt. Above it, accuracy went from single digits to 50%+.

The pretraining loss curve through that range was smooth — no kink, no phase transition. Cross-entropy on web text fell predictably along the Chinchilla curve. But the downstream capability was discontinuous in the metric people cared about.

Why scaling laws missed it: the law fits average loss over a broad distribution. Multi-step reasoning sits in a thin slice of that distribution where being slightly-better-than-chance and being-actually-correct are separated by a threshold — you need every step right, so per-step accuracy compounds multiplicatively. Smooth improvements in per-token loss can produce sharp improvements in full-trace correctness once per-step accuracy crosses ~90%.

The honest follow-up: Schaeffer et al. (2023, "Are Emergent Abilities a Mirage?") argued many "emergent" capabilities are artifacts of nonlinear / discontinuous metrics (exact-match, multi-step accuracy). Switch to token-level log-prob and the discontinuity often smooths out. So "scaling laws didn't predict it" is partly true (the *metric* people cared about jumped) and partly a measurement artifact (the underlying competence was rising smoothly). Both can be true: the loss law was right about loss; it was the wrong instrument for predicting when a model would *cross a usefulness threshold*.

Other candidates: instruction-following (jumped with RLHF, not pretraining scale, so arguably out of scope), tool use, theory-of-mind benchmarks, code execution traces.

%%FILL: the gap between loss reduction and capability gain — when do they decouple%%

They decouple in three regimes:

**1. Threshold-gated capabilities.** When a task requires N sequential correct steps, end-to-end accuracy is roughly `p^N` where `p` is per-step accuracy. Loss falling from 2.0 → 1.8 nats might move `p` from 0.85 → 0.92 — invisible at N=1, dramatic at N=10. Long-horizon tasks (multi-step math, multi-file code edits, agentic tool use) sit far above the loss curve in apparent volatility because of this compounding. Loss is smooth; capability looks like a step function.

**2. Distribution mismatch between pretraining loss and evaluation.** Pretraining loss is averaged over web text. The evaluation distribution (MMLU, HumanEval, GPQA, agentic benchmarks) is a thin, weird slice. A model can lower its average loss by getting better at the bulk of the distribution (common phrasings, frequent topics) without moving on the tail where the eval lives. This is why two models with nearly identical loss can diverge by 10+ points on benchmarks — the loss is the same, but the *composition* of where the loss reduction happened is different. Data mix changes, quality filtering, and instruction data all exploit this.

**3. Post-training.** RLHF, DPO, instruction tuning, RLAIF, and constitutional methods change capability dramatically while barely moving pretraining-style loss (and sometimes raising it on raw web text). The scaling law's `L` is no longer the right axis. A 7B instruct model can outperform a 70B base model on most user-facing tasks despite worse perplexity on C4. The relevant scaling law for post-training (how capability scales with RLHF compute, preference-data size, reward-model quality) is much less well-characterized than pretraining and has its own — different — exponents.

Practical consequence: pretraining-loss scaling tells you what's *possible* at a given compute budget. It doesn't tell you what users will *experience*. The latter is gated by post-training, evaluation distribution, and which threshold-shaped capabilities you happen to need. Frontier labs increasingly report capability scaling (MMLU vs. compute, agentic-task success vs. compute) alongside loss scaling, because internally the loss number stopped being the planning instrument around the GPT-4 / Claude 2 era.

%%FILL: what happens when you scale data without scaling parameters (or vice versa) past compute-optimal%%

**Over-train (data >> compute-optimal D for given N):** Loss keeps falling, but with sharply diminishing returns per token. Llama 2 7B was trained on ~2T tokens — roughly 14× the Chinchilla-optimal allocation for that param count. Llama 3 8B went to ~15T tokens, ~100× over-trained. They're not compute-optimal — for the same training FLOPs you could train a bigger model to lower loss. But:

- **Inference economics flip the calculation.** Chinchilla optimizes training loss per training FLOP. If you serve the model billions of times, total cost is dominated by inference (params × tokens-generated × requests). A smaller, more-data-trained model is cheaper to *deploy* even though it cost more to *train per unit loss*. Llama 3 8B vs. a hypothetical Chinchilla-optimal 30B at the same loss: the 8B is ~4× cheaper to serve forever.
- **Quality-per-param keeps rising past optimal.** The loss curve flattens but doesn't plateau. The B/D^β term keeps shrinking; you just pay more compute per nat than you would by adding params. In the data-constrained regime, repeating tokens past ~4 epochs starts hurting.
- **Risk: data exhaustion and contamination.** Scraping more high-quality tokens gets exponentially harder past a few trillion. Eval-set leakage rises with corpus size. Synthetic data fills the gap but introduces its own scaling pathologies (mode collapse, narrowing distribution).

**Over-parameterize (N >> compute-optimal for given D):** This was the pre-Chinchilla failure mode. GPT-3 175B on 300B tokens was ~10× over-parameterized for its data. Symptoms:

- Loss is worse than a smaller, better-trained model at the same compute.
- The model is harder to align, harder to fine-tune (fewer tokens means weaker grounding of behaviors), and more prone to memorization of the limited data it did see.
- Inference cost is ruinous relative to capability — you pay 175B-param latency for sub-Chinchilla loss.
- Returns on additional params nearly flatten — `A/N^α` is already small; pushing N further moves loss negligibly while compute and serving cost scale linearly.

**The asymmetry that matters in practice:** post-Chinchilla, almost everyone deliberately *over-trains* relative to compute-optimal because inference economics dominate. Almost no one deliberately *over-parameterizes* anymore — that's the pure-loss mistake the field already made and corrected. The real frontier question is how far past Chinchilla-optimal you can push D before quality-of-data effects (repetition, contamination, distribution narrowing) overwhelm the capability gain. Empirically that ceiling keeps moving up as filtering and synthetic-data techniques improve, but it's not infinite.

%%FILL: a published scaling-law claim that's been quietly walked back%%

The clearest case: **Kaplan et al.'s param/data allocation itself.** Not retracted, but functionally superseded by Chinchilla — and the lab that published it (OpenAI) shipped GPT-3 on the now-known-wrong recipe and never publicly issued an "we got this wrong" note. The walk-back happened by collective practice: every flagship model since 2022 follows Chinchilla-or-beyond allocations, and the original Kaplan exponents are no longer cited as the operating recipe even by their authors.

The proximate cause was specific and unflattering: Kaplan's sweep used a learning-rate schedule that didn't fully decay over the longer training runs, so longer-trained models looked artificially worse than they should have, biasing the fit toward "params matter more." That's a methodology bug, not a deep finding about transformers — but the field built training plans on top of it for two years.

A second, more recent and still-in-progress walk-back: **the "data is effectively unlimited" framing implicit in Chinchilla.** The Chinchilla paper assumed single-epoch training on a corpus large enough to feed any compute budget. By 2023–2024, frontier labs were visibly running out of high-quality unique tokens. Villalobos et al. (Epoch, 2022, updated 2024, "Will We Run Out of Data?") projected high-quality text exhaustion around 2026–2032. Muennighoff et al. (2023) showed how the law modifies under repetition. Nobody retracted Chinchilla — but "scale data with compute" stopped being a trivially-available lever, and the field pivoted hard to synthetic data, quality filtering, and curriculum without ever publishing a clean "Chinchilla under data constraints" follow-up that everyone agrees on.

A third, smaller one: **specific "emergent capability" claims from the Wei et al. emergence paper (2022).** Schaeffer et al. (2023) showed several of those curves smooth out under different metrics. The original paper hasn't been retracted and the emergence framing is still widely used, but in technical conversation among researchers, the strong form ("capabilities appear discontinuously at threshold scales") has been softened to "some metrics make smooth underlying improvement look discontinuous; some genuine threshold effects remain but fewer than originally claimed."

Honorable mention: **Kaplan's claim that architecture details barely matter for the loss curve.** Held up reasonably well within dense decoder-only transformers, but MoE, mixture-of-depths, state-space models, and long-context architectures all have meaningfully different exponents. The "architecture-invariant" framing was overstated and is rarely defended in that strong form now.
