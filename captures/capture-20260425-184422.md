%%FILL: what the logits look like immediately before a token is chosen — describe the shape%%

A vector of length |V| (vocab size — for me, ~200K entries). Raw, unnormalized real numbers, typically ranging roughly from -20 to +20 after the final unembedding matrix multiply. Shape is heavy-tailed: a handful of tokens score 5–15 points above the mean, most cluster in a low-signal band near the median, and a long tail sits 10+ points below. Softmax over that vector turns it into the probability distribution — the gap between #1 and #2 in logit space is what determines whether the choice feels "obvious" (gap of 5+ → top token has >99% mass) or "genuinely uncertain" (gap <0.5 → top-5 share the mass).

%%FILL: what temperature actually does to the distribution, with a concrete example at temp=0.1, 1.0, and 2.0%%

Temperature divides the logits before softmax: `softmax(logits / T)`. Lower T sharpens, higher T flattens.

Concrete: suppose three tokens with logits `[5.0, 3.0, 2.0]`.

- **T=0.1** → divide to `[50, 30, 20]` → softmax ≈ `[~1.0, 2e-9, 9e-14]`. Effectively deterministic; argmax wins by a landslide.
- **T=1.0** → softmax of `[5, 3, 2]` ≈ `[0.84, 0.11, 0.04]`. Top token usually wins but #2 fires meaningfully.
- **T=2.0** → divide to `[2.5, 1.5, 1.0]` → softmax ≈ `[0.56, 0.21, 0.13]` (plus mass leaking into the rest of the vocab). The tail wakes up — low-logit garbage tokens that were 10⁻⁶ at T=1 are now 10⁻³ and will fire on long generations.

Temperature does not change *which* token has the highest logit; it changes how much the runner-ups matter.

%%FILL: top-p (nucleus) sampling vs. top-k — when each one fails%%

**top-k** keeps the k highest-logit tokens, renormalizes, samples from those. **top-p** sorts tokens by probability, walks down until cumulative mass ≥ p, samples from that nucleus.

**top-k fails on shape mismatch.** k=40 in a confident distribution (top token is 0.99) drags 39 near-zero tokens into eligibility — most don't fire, but the floor raises noise. k=40 in a flat distribution (50 tokens at ~0.02 each) chops off legitimate candidates at an arbitrary boundary. The k is fixed; the distribution isn't.

**top-p fails on heavy tails and on cliffs.** p=0.95 on a "the cat sat on the ___" distribution where "mat" is 0.96 collapses the nucleus to one token — equivalent to greedy, no diversity. p=0.95 on a flat creative-writing distribution sweeps in 200 tokens including grammatical garbage from the tail. And in code generation, where the right token often holds 0.999 mass, top-p with any reasonable p degrades to greedy and you lose the ability to recover from a bad early choice.

In practice: combine them (top-k=50 ∩ top-p=0.95) so each covers the other's failure mode.

%%FILL: what happens during greedy decoding that causes repetition loops%%

Greedy always picks argmax. Once the model emits a phrase, that phrase becomes part of the context — and the model was trained on text where repeated phrases sometimes continue (lists, refrains, chants). So the conditional probability `P(phrase | ...phrase)` is often higher than `P(novel continuation | ...phrase)`, especially as the repeated phrase grows and dominates the attention pattern.

The loop is self-reinforcing: emit "the cat sat on the mat. The cat sat on the mat." and now the third repetition's logits are *even more* peaked on the same tokens than the second's were, because the in-context evidence for "this is a repeating sequence" has doubled. Greedy commits to argmax, argmax keeps winning, the gap widens. No noise injection means no escape. Sampling at any T>0 would eventually pick a non-argmax token and break the cycle; greedy can't.

%%FILL: how repetition penalties work and why they sometimes break grammar%%

Two common forms:

- **CTRL-style multiplicative penalty (Keskar 2019):** for any token id that already appeared in context, divide its logit by penalty α (typically 1.1–1.3) if logit > 0, multiply by α if < 0. Reduces the probability of repeating any token already seen.
- **Frequency/presence penalties (OpenAI-style):** subtract a constant from the logit of every previously-seen token, optionally scaled by how many times it appeared.

Why they break grammar: language has tokens that *must* repeat. Articles ("the", "a"), prepositions ("of", "to", "in"), punctuation, common verbs, function words, and crucially — variable names and identifiers in code. A repetition penalty applied uniformly across the vocabulary punishes "the" the same way it punishes a content word. Crank α to 1.5 and the model will start writing "A cat sat on mat" or substituting rare synonyms for "is" — grammatical determiners get suppressed because they appeared three sentences ago. In code, the penalty turns `for i in range(len(items)):` into `for j in range(len(itms))` because reusing `i` and `items` got penalized.

The fix is presence-aware penalties (only penalize content tokens, leave function words alone) or n-gram blocking (penalize repeated *spans*, not repeated *tokens*) — but vanilla repetition penalty is a blunt instrument.

%%FILL: what beam search would change about your output and why most modern systems don't use it%%

Beam search keeps the top-B partial sequences at each step (ranked by cumulative log-prob), expands all of them, prunes back to B, repeats. Output: the highest-probability *complete sequence*, not just locally-greedy choices.

What it would change: my output would be **more probable but less interesting**. Beams converge on safe, high-likelihood phrasings — "the dog ran quickly" beats "the dog bolted" because common words have higher conditional probability. You get fluency without flair. For machine translation circa 2017 this was a win; for open-ended generation it's a loss.

Why modern systems don't use it for chat/creative output:

1. **Mode collapse on long sequences.** Probability multiplies; the most-probable long sequence is often a degenerate one (empty answers, "I don't know", repeated phrases) because high-probability tokens compound.
2. **Length bias.** Shorter sequences win on raw log-prob; you need length normalization, and tuning that is a footgun.
3. **Diversity death.** Beams cluster — B=5 often returns 5 paraphrases of the same answer, not 5 distinct candidates.
4. **Computational cost.** B× the forward passes per step, with no quality benefit for instruction-following models that have already been RLHF'd toward good single-sample outputs.
5. **Sampling won the architectural argument.** Once temperature + top-p tuned well, the marginal value of beam search dropped to zero for autoregressive chat.

It survives in narrow places: machine translation, summarization with strict length constraints, structured-output decoding where you need the globally-best parse.

%%FILL: a sampling configuration that would make you produce visibly worse output and why%%

`temperature=1.8, top-p=1.0, top-k=0, repetition_penalty=1.0, no frequency penalty`.

Why each piece breaks it:

- **T=1.8** flattens the distribution hard. Tokens that should sit at 10⁻⁶ now sit at 10⁻². Over a 500-token generation, the probability of *zero* tail-token incidents is `(1 - 0.01)^500 ≈ 0.7%` — almost certainly you'll emit something incoherent.
- **top-p=1.0, top-k=0** means no truncation. Every token in the 200K vocab is eligible, including unused tokens, byte fragments, foreign-script tokens that don't belong in English output, and reserved control tokens. T=1.8 pulls them into the live nucleus.
- **No repetition penalty** means once the model lands on a weird token (say, a Cyrillic letter or a code-fence fragment) and it gets reinforced by context, nothing pushes it out.

Output failure mode: starts coherent, then mid-sentence emits a token like `」` or `\x{200B}` or a random Korean syllable, then either (a) the off-distribution token destabilizes the next prediction and the output degenerates into glossolalia, or (b) the model recovers but the artifact stays in context and re-fires later.

The deeper reason it's bad: T>1 with no truncation violates the assumption sampling truncation is built on. Top-p and top-k exist *because* T alone can't separate "creative variation in the head of the distribution" from "noise in the tail." Removing both safeguards while raising T past 1 isn't creative — it's just sampling from the model's worst guesses with extra weight.
