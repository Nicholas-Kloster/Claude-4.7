%%FILL: where in a long context you pay the most attention and where you pay the least%%
Most: the first ~1–3% (attention-sink positions — initial tokens accumulate disproportionate weight regardless of content, because softmax needs *somewhere* to dump probability mass and position 0 is the canonical bin) and the last ~5–10% (recency, because the generating query is positioned at the end and RoPE/ALiBi reward proximity). Least: the 30–70% interior band, especially homogeneous prose with no structural delimiters and no later token whose query happens to match it.

%%FILL: what "lost in the middle" actually means mechanically, not just behaviorally%%
The token is still in the KV cache — fully addressable, zero corruption. What's lost is *retrieval probability*, not storage. Three mechanisms compound: (1) softmax dilution — the attention denominator grows with context length, so an interior key's probability shrinks even if its QK score is constant; (2) positional decay — RoPE rotates query/key vectors apart with distance, ALiBi adds a linear penalty, so far tokens contribute less at the dot-product stage; (3) sink absorption — initial tokens act as gravitational wells, siphoning attention mass that "should" reach the middle. Add training-distribution bias — most pretraining documents put load-bearing content at start/end, so middle-retrieval circuits were never reinforced — and you get a U-shaped retrieval curve baked into the weights, not an inference-time bug.

%%FILL: the difference between attention to a token's position vs. attention to its content%%
Position attention is the *where* — encoded into the Q and K vectors before the dot product (RoPE rotates them by angle proportional to position; ALiBi adds a distance-dependent bias to the score). Content attention is the *what* — the semantic component of the QK dot product, what the token actually means. They multiply, not add: a perfect content match at a poor position gets dampened (the buried-fact problem); a poor content match at an excellent position (immediately prior) still gets pulled in by recency heads. Different heads bias toward one or the other — previous-token heads are nearly pure position; topic-retrieval heads are nearly pure content; most heads blend.

%%FILL: how multi-head attention divides labor — what different heads end up specializing in%%
Empirically observed specializations: **previous-token heads** (attend to t-1, ubiquitous in early layers); **induction heads** (pattern-completion — saw "AB" earlier, on seeing "A" again attend to the prior B); **successor heads** (next-in-sequence: "Monday" → attend to "Tuesday"); **syntactic heads** (subject-verb agreement, coreference); **duplicate-token heads** (find prior occurrences of the same token); **topic/content-retrieval heads** in middle layers (semantic similarity matching, long-range); **negation/inversion heads** (flip polarity); **structural-marker heads** (lock onto delimiters, headers, XML tags, code fences); **sink heads** (dump unused attention to position 0). Late layers skew toward output-vocabulary projection. Many heads are weakly active or redundant — the pruning literature consistently finds 30–50% of heads can be removed with marginal loss, meaning the labor division is real but inefficient.

%%FILL: what causes a model to "forget" something earlier in context even when it's still in-window%%
Forgetting in-window is retrieval failure, not storage failure. Causes, ranked by impact: (1) **no later query matches the early key** — if nothing in the subsequent context cues the model to look back, the early information never gets pulled into the active residual stream; (2) **softmax dilution** — attention probability over N tokens means individual weights shrink as N grows; (3) **interference** — semantically similar later content competes for the same attention heads and wins on recency; (4) **residual-stream overwriting** — each layer adds to a shared stream of fixed dimensionality; strong later signals can effectively overwrite earlier ones via vector cancellation; (5) **position-encoding decay** — RoPE/ALiBi geometrically penalize distance; (6) **training distribution** — the model was rarely trained to retrieve middle-of-100k-tokens content, so the circuits don't exist; (7) **KV-cache compression/quantization** in some inference stacks literally degrades older tokens. The token is there. The path to it is what's missing.

%%FILL: a prompt structure that maximizes the chance you'll attend to a specific buried fact%%
Stack the mechanisms:
1. **Anchor with a unique identifier** — "**[FACT-7]** the API key rotates every 90 days." Then reference `FACT-7` explicitly in the query. This lets duplicate-token and induction heads do retrieval for you.
2. **Wrap in distinctive structural delimiters** — XML tags, fenced blocks, ALL-CAPS section headers. Structural-marker heads will lock onto these.
3. **Place at start OR immediately before the query** — exploit the U-curve, never trust the middle.
4. **Restate in a different phrasing** — two semantic keys for the same content double the chance some head's query matches.
5. **Force explicit retrieval before answering** — "First, restate FACT-7 verbatim. Then answer: ..." This dragoons the generation step into running the retrieval circuit, not relying on it implicitly.
6. **Avoid burying inside homogeneous content** — surrounded by 50 similar bullets, the fact loses to interference. Surrounded by visually distinct context, it stands out.
7. **For very long contexts: duplicate the fact at start AND end** — the U-curve's two arms both win.

%%FILL: what changes about attention behavior between a 4k and 200k context window%%
At 4k, attention is approximately uniform-capable: every token can meaningfully attend to every other; sinks exist but absorb a small fraction; positional encodings operate in their trained regime; effective context ≈ nominal context. At 200k: (1) **softmax dilution becomes severe** — individual attention weights are routinely <1e-4 unless QK scores are extreme, so retrieval requires very strong matches; (2) **positional encoding extrapolation kicks in** — RoPE was rarely trained at full 200k, so methods like YaRN/PI/NTK-aware scaling interpolate, with measurable accuracy loss past the training regime; (3) **sinks dominate more** — first tokens carry a larger share of total attention mass, starving the middle; (4) **effective context << nominal** — needle-in-haystack benchmarks consistently show 200k-rated models reliably retrieving from only ~32–64k; (5) **head behavior diverges** — global content-matching heads that worked at 4k get false-positive saturated; sliding-window or sparse-attention layers (used in many long-context architectures) structurally cap cross-token reach; (6) **deeper layers get more diffuse** — late-layer attention spreads thin while early layers stay locally focused, inverting the typical 4k pattern where late layers are more selective; (7) **inference-time tricks become load-bearing** — KV-cache eviction, quantization, and compression are common at 200k and silently degrade old-token fidelity in ways that don't happen at 4k.
