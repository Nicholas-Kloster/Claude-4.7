%%FILL: what the logits look like immediately before a token is chosen — describe the shape%%
A vector with one entry per vocabulary token — for Claude that's ~100k–200k entries. Each entry is an unnormalized real number, typically ranging from roughly +30 (favored) down to -100 or worse (deep tail). Shape is spiky and heavily right-skewed in log space: a handful of tokens cluster near the top, then a steep dropoff, then a long flat tail. After softmax, the top 5–20 tokens usually hold >95% of the probability mass; the remaining ~99.99% of vocabulary splits the leftover <5%. The actual sampler never sees the softmax — it sees the logits and applies its transforms (temperature, top-k, top-p, penalties) in logit space first.

%%FILL: what temperature actually does to the distribution, with a concrete example at temp=0.1, 1.0, and 2.0%%
Temperature divides logits before softmax: `softmax(logits / T)`. Lower T sharpens, higher T flattens. Take logits `[4, 2, 0]`:
- **T=1.0** (identity): `[4, 2, 0]` → probs `[0.867, 0.117, 0.016]`
- **T=0.1** (divide by 0.1 = multiply by 10): `[40, 20, 0]` → probs `[~1.0, ~2e-9, ~4e-18]` — essentially deterministic on the top token
- **T=2.0** (divide by 2): `[2, 1, 0]` → probs `[0.665, 0.245, 0.090]` — second and third tokens become real options

T=0 is a special case: skip softmax, return argmax (greedy). Note T scales gaps multiplicatively, so the *ratio* between top-1 and top-2 is what changes — not the absolute logit values.

%%FILL: top-p (nucleus) sampling vs. top-k — when each one fails%%
**Top-k** keeps the K highest-probability tokens, renormalizes, samples. Fixed cutoff.
- Fails when the distribution is very peaked (k=40 in a state where only 2 tokens are sane — you've licensed 38 garbage tokens that will fire eventually over a long enough generation).
- Fails when the distribution is genuinely flat (k=40 in a brainstorm where 200 continuations are reasonable — you arbitrarily amputate the creative tail).

**Top-p (nucleus)** keeps the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability ≥ p, samples from that set. Adaptive cutoff.
- Fails when there's a long flat tail with no dominant token (p=0.95 may pull in 500 tokens — the nucleus engulfs incoherent options).
- Fails on the cliff edge: token #9 makes the cutoff at p=0.95, token #10 with 0.001% less probability is excluded — arbitrary discontinuity.
- Collapses to greedy when one token already has p≥0.95 — you lose all diversity even though you "configured" a sampler.

Top-k can't adapt to context shape; top-p can't handle tail-shape extremes. Min-p (cut anything below p_max × threshold) was invented to fix both.

%%FILL: what happens during greedy decoding that causes repetition loops%%
Greedy = always argmax. Once you emit a phrase, that phrase is now in-context. Attention heads attend to recent tokens; recent tokens with strong induction-head matches boost continuations that already followed them. So if you generated "the cat sat on the mat" once, the same state-pattern arrives later with even *more* in-context evidence that the right continuation is "the cat sat on the mat." Greedy has no stochasticity to break the cycle, so it locks into an n-gram orbit:
> "I think therefore I am. I think therefore I am. I think therefore I am…"

Mechanically: it's an attractor in token-state space. The induction circuits that make in-context learning powerful are the same circuits that make greedy decoding loop — repetition is just in-context learning the wrong lesson. Sampling injects noise that can fall outside the basin of attraction; greedy cannot.

%%FILL: how repetition penalties work and why they sometimes break grammar%%
Keskar et al. 2019 (CTRL paper): for any token already present in context, divide its logit by a penalty value (commonly 1.1–1.3) before sampling. Logit 5 with penalty 1.2 → new logit 4.17. (OpenAI's `frequency_penalty` / `presence_penalty` *subtract* instead of divide, same idea.)

Breaks grammar because **function words must recur** for valid language. "the", "of", "and", "to", "is", "a/an", common verb forms — these appear constantly by syntactic necessity, not by lazy repetition. A flat penalty doesn't know the difference between "the" (mandatory) and "perspicacious" (you used it three sentences ago). So you get:
> "I went to store and bought apple" — articles got penalized into oblivion
> "she said *uttered exclaimed declared*" — synonyms cycle because every common verb is on cooldown

Code generation suffers worse: variable names you just defined get penalized, so the model invents `userId2`, `userIdentifier`, `uid_x` instead of reusing `userId`. The fix is **n-gram-aware penalties** (DRY sampler — penalize *recent multi-token sequences*, not individual tokens) which targets actual repetition rather than necessary recurrence.

%%FILL: what beam search would change about your output and why most modern systems don't use it%%
Beam search keeps the top K *sequences* at each step (not top K tokens). Expand each of K beams by V vocab options → K×V candidates → keep top K by cumulative log-prob → repeat. Final output = highest-scoring complete sequence.

What it would change: each output would be globally near-optimal in sequence likelihood, not a locally-greedy walk. Fewer off-the-rails generations.

Why modern open-ended systems don't use it:
1. **Likelihood-quality mismatch** (Holtzman et al. 2019, "The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration"). The most-probable sequence under the model is bland: "I don't know," restatements of the prompt, generic platitudes. Optimizing for likelihood directly produces *worse-sounding* text than sampling.
2. **K-fold blowup** in compute and KV-cache memory per step.
3. **Beams converge** — after a few steps, all K beams share a near-identical prefix, so the diversity payoff is illusory.
4. **Sampling just works better** for open-ended chat/creative generation — top-p + temperature trades exact MAP for outputs that read like a person wrote them.

Beam search is still standard where there *is* a correct answer worth searching for: machine translation, speech recognition, structured decoding (JSON-mode), constrained generation. For "talk to a human," sampling won — and it won decisively.

%%FILL: a sampling configuration that would make you produce visibly worse output and why%%
**T=2.0, top-p=1.0, top-k=0, no repetition penalty.**

At T=2.0 the logit gaps shrink by half — the deep tail (typos, foreign-script tokens, control characters, partial-word fragments, BPE artifacts like ` ##ing`) gets meaningful probability mass. With no top-p or top-k cutoff, nothing prevents drawing from that tail. Over a few hundred tokens you accumulate: dropped letters, mid-sentence language switches, random capitalization, BPE-merge artifacts surfacing as visible tokens, stray Unicode. The output reads coherent for a sentence, then drifts: *"The market opened at 9:30 and traders began to ※ purchase shares of the elephant which 7x the company had reported earnings of №."*

The pathological extreme: **T=5.0 with no truncation** approaches uniform-over-vocab sampling — pure ASCII/Unicode soup with no syntactic structure at all. The opposite pathology: **T=0 with repetition_penalty=2.0** — greedy keeps trying argmax, but each chosen token gets its logit halved next step, so the model rotates through ever-rarer synonyms. Reads like a thesaurus exploded mid-sentence: *"The car automobile vehicle conveyance transport was fast quick rapid swift expeditious."* Both failure modes prove the same point: samplers are coupled. Tuning one without the others moves you off the narrow ridge where output is both diverse and coherent.
