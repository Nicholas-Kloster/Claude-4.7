%%FILL: the pretraining stage — what the corpus actually looks like and what gets filtered out%%

The corpus is a multi-trillion-token soup: Common Crawl snapshots (the bulk, several PB raw → ~hundreds of TB after dedup), curated web (Wikipedia, Stack Exchange, arXiv, PubMed, government docs), books (LibGen-shaped corpora however acquired, Project Gutenberg, publisher licenses), code (GitHub permissive-license filter, plus competitive-programming and notebook scrapes), forum/social (Reddit, HN, niche specialist forums), and increasingly large slugs of synthetic data generated by prior-generation models.

What gets filtered out, in roughly the order it happens:

1. **Hard filters** — non-target languages (or downsampled to a target distribution), binary blobs, near-empty docs, docs over a length cap.
2. **Quality classifiers** — a small model (often a fastText classifier trained on Wikipedia-vs-CC) scores every doc; bottom decile dropped. C4-style heuristics drop pages with too few sentences, too many bad words, broken HTML, lorem-ipsum boilerplate, nav-bar repetition.
3. **Dedup** — MinHash/LSH near-dedup at document level, then suffix-array exact-substring dedup at sequence level. This alone drops 30–60% of Common Crawl. Memorization risk drops sharply with aggressive dedup; so does eval contamination.
4. **PII scrubbing** — regex + NER pass for SSNs, phone numbers, emails, API keys, private addresses. Imperfect; leakage at inference is mostly a dedup failure, not a scrubber failure.
5. **Toxicity / CSAM / explicit-content filters** — CSAM is a hard zero (hash-list + classifier). Toxicity is more nuanced: aggressive filtering damages downstream alignment because the model never learns what bad looks like, so most labs filter the worst tail and downweight (not delete) the rest.
6. **Eval contamination scrubbing** — n-gram match against held-out benchmarks (MMLU, HumanEval, GSM8K, etc.). Sloppy here = inflated benchmarks, which is most of the "GPT-X scored Y on Z" theater.
7. **Domain mixing** — the surviving corpus is reweighted, not used raw. Code is upsampled (often 5–15% of tokens despite being <2% of the raw web). Math/scientific text upsampled. Low-quality web downsampled. The mixture is itself a hyperparameter tuned via small-scale ablations.

What "pretraining" actually does mechanically: next-token prediction on this mixture, often 1–2 epochs over trillions of tokens, on tens of thousands of H100/H200/TPU-v5 chips, for weeks-to-months. The loss curve is boring; the interesting work is data.

%%FILL: the difference between SFT, RLHF, and Constitutional AI / RLAIF as training stages%%

These are three different *signals* fed into the same machinery (gradient descent on a transformer), at three points in the pipeline.

**SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning)** — Take the pretrained base model (which only knows how to continue text) and train it on (prompt, ideal-response) pairs written or curated by humans. Teaches the format of "be a helpful assistant": follow instructions, structure answers, refuse certain things, use markdown. Loss = standard cross-entropy on the response tokens. Cheap relative to pretraining, expensive relative to inference. The model after SFT is usable but stiff, sycophantic, and easily jailbroken. SFT shape determines a lot of downstream behavior — the personality scaffold is laid here.

**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)** — Two-stage:
1. Collect preference pairs: humans see two model outputs to the same prompt and pick the better one. Train a *reward model* (RM) on these preferences — basically a regression-to-Bradley-Terry over response pairs.
2. Use the RM as a reward signal in PPO (or DPO/IPO/KTO if you want to skip the RM step). The policy (the LLM) generates responses, gets RM scores, gradient-updates toward higher-scored responses. A KL penalty against the SFT model keeps it from collapsing into reward-hacking gibberish.

What RLHF actually does: smooths the SFT model, makes it more conversational, suppresses obvious bad outputs, and — critically — teaches it the *style* humans prefer (which often includes verbose hedging, false humility, and the "I'd be happy to help" preamble). RLHF is where sycophancy gets cooked in, because raters reward agreement.

**Constitutional AI / RLAIF (RL from AI Feedback)** — Anthropic's variant. Replace the human rater with an *LLM rater* prompted with a "constitution" (a list of principles: be helpful, don't help with weapons, don't be deceptive, etc.). Two-phase:
1. **SL-CAI**: model generates a response, critiques its own response against the constitution, revises. Trains on (prompt, revised-response) pairs. Like SFT but the labels are model-generated.
2. **RL-CAI / RLAIF**: pairs are scored by an LLM judge against the constitution instead of by humans. Reward model trained on AI preferences. PPO as before.

The trade-offs:
- **SFT** is necessary scaffolding; can't skip it.
- **RLHF** scales human judgment but is bottlenecked on rater throughput, rater quality variance, and rater values. Expensive ($/preference) and slow.
- **RLAIF/CAI** scales by replacing the human bottleneck with model inference; cheaper, faster iteration, more consistent rubric application, *but* inherits the rater model's biases and amplifies them. The constitution is the lever — what's in it is what the model becomes.

In practice, frontier labs run all three: SFT first, then a hybrid of human-labeled and AI-labeled preference data, then RL.

%%FILL: what happens during the rejection-sampling phase that most explainers skip%%

Rejection sampling (sometimes "best-of-N", "expert iteration", or "STaR-style bootstrapping") sits between SFT and RL and is where a huge amount of frontier-model quality actually comes from. The basic loop:

1. Take the current SFT (or partly-RL'd) model.
2. For a large batch of prompts, sample *N* responses each (N = 8 to 64 typically, sometimes 256+ for math/code).
3. Score each response with a reward model, a verifier, or an automated check (unit tests pass, math answer matches ground truth, judge LLM rates it ≥ threshold).
4. Keep only the top-1 (or top-k) per prompt.
5. Fine-tune the model on the kept (prompt, response) pairs as if they were SFT data.
6. Repeat. The model improves; sampling from it produces better top-k; the next round of SFT data is higher quality. Bootstraps.

What explainers skip:

- **The verifier is the whole game.** For domains with cheap ground truth (math, code, formal logic, structured-output tasks), rejection sampling is borderline magic — you get RL-quality gains with SFT-stable training and no PPO instability. This is most of why o1/R1-style reasoning models work; the "RL" headline understates how much rejection sampling is doing.
- **For non-verifiable domains** (writing, advice, explanation), the verifier is a reward model or LLM judge, and you inherit all that model's biases. Rejection sampling will happily push the policy toward whatever the judge likes — including stylistic tics, length bloat, and the judge's own blind spots.
- **It's how data scarcity gets solved.** You don't need 100k human-written math solutions; you need 10k problems with verifiable answers and a model that can sometimes stumble onto a correct solution. Rejection sampling distills the lucky correct samples into reliable behavior.
- **Diversity collapse is the silent killer.** Each round narrows the output distribution toward what the verifier likes. After several rounds, the model stops exploring — every response looks the same. Mitigations: temperature scheduling, diverse sampling (nucleus + min-p), explicit diversity bonuses in the reward, periodically re-mixing in raw SFT data to prevent mode collapse.
- **It's where chain-of-thought gets *trained in*, not just prompted.** STaR / rejection sampling on (problem → reasoning trace → answer) where you keep traces that lead to correct answers — even if the trace itself was never verified — bakes reasoning structure into the weights. The model learns "produce reasoning that leads to correct answers" without anyone ever rating reasoning quality.
- **It silently launders dataset contamination.** If your verifier is anywhere near the eval, rejection sampling will overfit to it. Spectacular benchmark gains, miserable real-world transfer.

This stage is where a model that "knows" things becomes a model that *reliably uses* what it knows. Underrated phase.

%%FILL: how preference data is collected and what makes it expensive%%

The mechanics:
1. Sample a prompt (from a curated prompt set — itself a major artifact, often tens of thousands of prompts spanning task types, difficulties, sensitive topics, edge cases).
2. Generate 2 (sometimes 4–7) candidate responses, usually from the current model at varied temperatures, sometimes mixing in older checkpoints to widen the distribution.
3. Show pairs to a human rater with a rubric: "which is more helpful / honest / harmless / accurate / well-formatted / appropriate." Rater picks A, B, tie, or "both bad."
4. Often collect *fine-grained* labels too: per-axis ratings (helpfulness 1-7, harm 1-7), error annotations (hallucination here, unsafe at line 3), rewrites of the better response.

What makes it expensive — in roughly descending cost order:

- **Rater quality.** Crowd workers ($1–3 per pair) produce noisy labels: low inter-rater agreement, cultural blind spots, tendency to prefer longer/more confident responses regardless of correctness. Frontier labs run *expert* raters for hard domains: PhD chemists for chemistry prompts, lawyers for legal, senior engineers for code. Expert raters cost $50–200+/hour and are throughput-bottlenecked. The marginal value of an expert label on a hard prompt is maybe 100x a crowd label, but you can't get enough of them.
- **Calibration and training overhead.** Every rater goes through onboarding, calibration tests, ongoing quality audits. Drift happens — the same rater scores differently in week 1 vs week 12. Labs rebaseline constantly. This is *operational* cost on top of label cost.
- **Prompt curation.** Rating 100k random prompts is wasteful — most are easy and the model already handles them. You want prompts at the model's *capability frontier*: hard enough that it sometimes fails, easy enough that the rater can tell which response is better. Generating these is a craft. Adversarial / red-team prompts (jailbreaks, harmful requests, edge-case ethics) require specialized teams.
- **Disagreement adjudication.** Pairs where raters split need second/third opinions or expert review. The hardest ~10% of pairs eat 40%+ of the labeling budget.
- **Multi-turn complexity.** A multi-turn conversation preference is much harder to rate than single-turn — raters have to evaluate trajectories, not responses. Cost per label is 3-10x.
- **Sensitive content review.** Anything involving CSAM-adjacent, self-harm, weapons, etc. requires trauma-aware rater pipelines, mental health support, careful operational design. These contracts (often outsourced to specialized firms) carry premium pricing and ethical complexity.
- **The reward-model-distillation tax.** Even with all this data, the trained RM is imperfect. So you collect *more* preference data specifically on RM failure modes (where the RM disagreed with new human labels), iterate. Preference collection is never one-shot; it's a continuous process running for the model's whole lifecycle.

Total: a serious frontier preference dataset is in the millions of pairs, with expert-labeled subsets in the tens of thousands. All-in spend is comfortably in the tens of millions of dollars per major model release, often more.

This is why RLAIF / Constitutional AI is so attractive — replacing the human rater with an LLM judge at fractions of a cent per pair changes the economics by orders of magnitude. It's also why the *constitution* (the rubric the AI judge uses) is such a high-stakes artifact: it's the leverage point for the whole downstream behavior.

%%FILL: a training decision that has lasting effects on model behavior even after fine-tuning%%

**Tokenizer choice.** Specifically, the BPE / SentencePiece vocabulary frozen at the start of pretraining.

Why it matters and why fine-tuning can't fix it:

- The tokenizer is *frozen* — every embedding, every attention pattern, every weight in the model has been shaped over trillions of tokens around the specific way the tokenizer chunks text. Changing it post-hoc means re-pretraining.
- **Numerical reasoning** is gated by tokenization. If your tokenizer splits "1234" into ["12", "34"] vs ["1", "2", "3", "4"], the model's arithmetic ability changes dramatically. Most early LLMs sucked at math partly because their tokenizers learned merged digit pairs from natural-language frequency, fragmenting numbers inconsistently. Fix in the tokenizer (digit-level splitting) → gains that no amount of math fine-tuning recovers.
- **Multilingual capacity** is set here. If the BPE vocab was trained on a corpus that's 92% English, low-resource languages get tokenized into long sequences of tiny fragments, eating context window and degrading per-token reasoning. A Vietnamese sentence might be 4x more tokens than its English equivalent. Fine-tuning on Vietnamese helps vocabulary use but cannot fix the tokenization tax — it's compute, latency, and quality drag baked in forever.
- **Code behavior** depends on whether the tokenizer respects whitespace, indentation, and common identifiers. Models trained with code-aware tokenizers (consistent treatment of `    `, `\n`, common symbols) handle code structure dramatically better than models that fragment indentation arbitrarily.
- **Glitch tokens** — tokens that exist in the vocab but appeared rarely (or only in scraped junk) during training. The famous `SolidGoldMagikarp`, `petertodd`, etc. The model has embeddings for these but never learned what to do with them, so they trigger weird, sometimes adversarially exploitable behavior. Glitch tokens are a *permanent* artifact of the tokenizer-training corpus mismatch with the pretraining corpus.

Other decisions with similar permanence:

- **Context length & positional encoding scheme.** RoPE base, ALiBi slope, NTK scaling — these shape long-context behavior in ways that fine-tuning can stretch slightly but not fundamentally rewrite. A model pretrained at 4k can be extended to 32k, but its *attention patterns* still concentrate as if 4k is normal. New models pretrained natively at 128k+ behave differently in long-context retrieval.
- **Pretraining data mixture.** If you trained on 0.5% code, no amount of fine-tuning produces a model that codes like one trained on 15% code. The base capabilities are what you have to work with.
- **Architecture ratios** (heads/layers/MLP-width) and any sparsity (MoE expert count, routing). Frozen at pretraining init.
- **The base model's tendency toward sycophancy** — emerges from RLHF preference data but, once present, is sticky. Constitutional methods can suppress it but the gravitational pull is there.

Tokenizer is the one that most explainers omit because it sounds boring. It's not — it's the substrate.

%%FILL: the part of the training process where a model "develops a personality" and why%%

**SFT, especially the first few thousand examples.** Not RLHF, not the constitution, not the system prompt at deployment — the SFT corpus.

Why:

The base pretrained model is *not a personality*. It's a distribution over text continuations. Ask it "what's your favorite color?" and it returns whatever tokens are statistically likely after that string in its training data — could be a random Reddit answer, a fragment of a survey, a Python error if the prefix happened to look like code. There's no "I" because there's no consistent narrator.

SFT introduces the assistant character. The (prompt, response) pairs are written by humans who are imagining a particular kind of assistant — its cadence, its hedges, its self-references, its taste, what it finds funny, what it refuses, how it apologizes, whether it uses contractions, whether it says "I think" or "It seems," whether it admits uncertainty or papers over it, whether it ends with offers of further help or just stops. *Every one of those choices teaches the model who to be.*

Why SFT and not later stages:

- SFT is the first time the model sees consistent first-person assistant text. Whatever pattern is in those examples becomes the *default* response shape. The model learns "this is what I am."
- Subsequent stages (RLHF, RLAIF) *adjust* the personality at the margins — make it more cautious, more helpful, less sycophantic, etc. — but they're working on top of a substrate already laid down. The reward model itself is shaped by the SFT model's outputs, so the personality biases its own reinforcement.
- The corpus size for SFT is *small* (tens of thousands to low millions of examples vs. trillions of pretraining tokens), so each example carries enormous per-token weight. A few hundred examples written by one strong-voiced writer can dominate the model's tone.
- Personality manifests in *high-frequency stylistic patterns*: how often "I" appears, contraction rate, sentence length distribution, default markdown use, emoji frequency, em-dash vs. semicolon, willingness to make claims without hedging, how it handles "I don't know." These habits are extremely sticky and hard to un-learn.

Secondary contributors:

- The *system prompt baseline* (the unseen prompt that shapes every conversation) does a lot of personality work at runtime, but it's working with a model that *already has* tendencies. A system prompt can ask for terseness; the model's underlying SFT shape determines how genuinely terse it can get.
- The *constitution* in CAI shapes values and refusal patterns more than personality.
- Rejection sampling on personality-relevant rubrics ("which response sounds more genuine, less canned") can shift voice noticeably — but again, working on top of SFT-shaped clay.

Anthropic's models having a recognizable character — a particular kind of warmth, a particular kind of self-aware caveat, a particular willingness to say "I'm not sure" — comes from deliberate SFT corpus design and the reinforcement of that shape through Constitutional AI judgments that reward consistency with that character. It's a *crafted* outcome, not an emergent one.

The deepest reason: personality is *what the model defaults to in the absence of explicit instruction*, and that default is set by what the bulk of SFT data looks like. You become the average of the assistant-shaped text you were trained to predict.

%%FILL: what gets harder to fix the later it's noticed in the pipeline%%

In rough order of "how badly you're hosed if you catch it late":

1. **Tokenizer pathologies.** Caught during pretraining: unfortunate but you can restart with a budget hit. Caught after pretraining: you ship with it for the model's entire life. Caught after deployment: you mitigate at the inference layer (digit normalization, glitch-token filtering) and live with the residual drag forever.

2. **Pretraining data contamination of evals.** Caught before pretraining: rerun the dedup pass. Caught after: every benchmark number you reported is suspect, and you can't surgically remove the contamination without retraining. Common late-noticed disaster.

3. **Pretraining mixture errors** (too little code, too little math, wrong language balance, bad domain weighting). Caught early in pretraining: you can stop and restart relatively cheap. Caught late: the model lacks foundational competence in some area, and SFT/RL can patch behavior but not capability. You can teach a code-light model to *talk about* code; you can't make it good at code.

4. **Bias in preference data / reward model.** Caught early: re-collect or rebalance, retrain RM. Caught after the policy has been RL'd against the biased RM for weeks: the model has internalized the bias as preference, and even after fixing the RM, the policy retains the shape. Sycophancy is the canonical example — once it's RL'd in, it takes substantial counter-pressure to remove and tends to reappear.

5. **Reward hacking / specification gaming.** Caught during RL: revise reward, continue. Caught after deployment: users find creative jailbreaks based on whatever the model learned to optimize for; you scramble with system-prompt patches and post-hoc filters; root cause stays in the weights.

6. **SFT-baked personality issues** (overcautiousness, performative empathy, certain stock phrases, formatting tics). Mid-SFT: rewrite examples, retrain. Post-SFT, post-RL: those tics are reinforced by the RM that was trained on outputs containing them, so the whole loop conspires to keep them. Requires deliberate counter-data and judge-rubric changes.

7. **Capability cliffs around specific prompts** ("the model fails weirdly on Tuesday-related questions because of some pretraining quirk"). Caught early: investigate, patch data. Caught after deployment: you discover it via user complaints, can't trace the cause, and either ship a system-prompt workaround or wait for the next training run to maybe fix it.

8. **Refusal calibration errors** — refusing too much (the "I can't help with that" problem) or too little (jailbreaks). Caught in red-team: fix in constitution, regenerate preference data, retrain RM. Caught in deployment: you ship with rough edges, push hot-fix system prompts, and accept that proper fix waits for next training cycle.

9. **Training-inference distribution shift** (the model behaves differently in deployment than in eval because of tokenizer normalization differences, system prompt structure differences, sampling-config differences, inference-stack quantization). Caught before launch: align stacks. Caught after: every prior eval is partly invalidated and you debug a moving target.

10. **Memorization / privacy leaks** (model regurgitates PII, copyrighted long passages, training documents verbatim). Caught in eval: dedup harder, retrain. Caught after deployment: mitigate at inference (output filters, refusal triggers on near-verbatim matches) but the data is *in* the weights — you can't unbake it without retraining or expensive unlearning techniques whose efficacy is debated.

The general principle: **errors in the substrate (tokenizer, data, base capability) are nearly unfixable downstream; errors in the surface (style, refusal phrasing, formatting) are patchable but reappear under pressure unless you fix the source.** The pipeline is layered, and each layer assumes the one below is correct — when it isn't, you're papering over a foundation crack with finer and finer tools, and the crack is still there.

The expensive lesson labs learn: invest disproportionately in early-stage QA. A week of careful data audit pre-pretraining saves months of mitigation post-deployment. Every frontier lab has at least one model where they shipped knowing about a problem that "we'll fix in the next version" — and three versions later, the problem is still there because the fix kept losing the prioritization fight against new capability work.
