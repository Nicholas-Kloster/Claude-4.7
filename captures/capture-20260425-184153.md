%%FILL: how the string "strawberry" is likely tokenized and why counting r's is hard for you%%
"strawberry" typically splits into ~2 tokens — something like `["str", "awberry"]` or `[" straw", "berry"]` depending on leading whitespace. The model never sees individual characters; it sees opaque integer IDs that map to multi-char chunks. Counting r's requires either (a) the training distribution to have hammered in the literal fact "strawberry has 3 r's," or (b) the model to internally decompose the token back into its UTF-8 bytes — which is a learned skill, not a primitive operation. The classic failure mode: the model "sees" `straw|berry`, mentally counts r's in each chunk separately, gets 1 + 1 = 2, and confidently answers wrong. The third r straddles the boundary in `straw` (the second r) but the model's spell-check is fuzzy on subword internals.

%%FILL: what happens to extremely long words like "antidisestablishmentarianism"%%
Splits into roughly 6–8 BPE tokens along common English morpheme-like boundaries: something like `["anti", "dis", "establish", "ment", "arian", "ism"]`. Modern tokenizers (tiktoken cl100k/o200k) handle this reasonably because the training corpus contains the constituent morphemes with high frequency, even if the full word is rare. The pathological case isn't long real words — it's long *novel* strings (random hex, base64 blobs, hashes), which fragment into many 1–3 byte tokens because no merge rules apply. A 64-char SHA256 can eat 30–50 tokens.

%%FILL: how Unicode characters outside Basic Multilingual Plane (emoji, CJK, math symbols) tokenize differently from ASCII%%
ASCII is byte-cheap: usually 1 char = 1 token or fractional. CJK is roughly 1 char = 1–2 tokens in modern tokenizers (much better than GPT-2's ~3–4× penalty, but still worse than ASCII). Emoji and supplementary-plane characters (U+10000+) require 4 UTF-8 bytes; if the specific emoji wasn't merged during BPE training, it shatters into 2–4 byte-fallback tokens. Skin-tone modifiers and ZWJ sequences (👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 = man+ZWJ+woman+ZWJ+girl+ZWJ+boy) tokenize as the *sequence*, often 6–10 tokens for a single rendered glyph. Mathematical alphanumerics (𝐀, 𝒜, 𝔸) almost always byte-fallback because they're rare in training — 4 bytes → 4 tokens each.

%%FILL: a specific input pattern that produces unexpected token boundaries%%
Repeated punctuation with whitespace: `" . . . . ."` tokenizes very differently from `"....."` — the first is one-token-per-`" ."`, the second collapses into a single learned `"....."` token. Another sharp edge: numbers. `"1234"` is often one token, `"12345"` two, `"1,234"` three, `"1 2 3 4"` four. This is why arithmetic accuracy varies wildly with formatting — `1234*5678` and `1,234 * 5,678` are *different problems* to the model. Also: trailing space before a stop token. `"Hello "` (with space) has a different final token than `"Hello"`, and if the next token expected is `"world"` vs `" world"`, you can end up with `Hello  world` (double space) or impossible-to-generate sequences.

%%FILL: how leading/trailing whitespace changes token IDs and why this matters for prompts%%
In BPE tokenizers, leading space is part of the token. `"hello"` (BOS) and `" hello"` (mid-sentence) are *different token IDs*. This causes the "trailing-space bug": if your prompt ends with `"Answer: "` (trailing space), the model's next token must be a no-leading-space variant, which is rarer in training and pushes probability mass onto weird candidates. Best practice — end prompts with `"Answer:"` (no trailing space) and let the model emit `" 42"`. Few-shot prompts also drift: if your examples end with `"\n"` and your final query ends with `" "`, you've created a distribution shift the model didn't see in training. Stop-sequence specifications hit the same wall — `stop=["\n"]` vs `stop=[" \n"]` vs `stop=["\n\n"]` are three different token-level conditions.

%%FILL: the byte-pair encoding decision that causes the most user-facing bugs%%
Greedy left-to-right merging without lookahead. BPE picks the longest matching merge at each position, which is locally optimal but globally fragile: insert one character and the entire downstream tokenization can shift. This breaks (a) caching — adding a single char invalidates KV cache from that point forward; (b) fine-grained edits — you can't reliably "replace one word" because the tokens before and after may renegotiate; (c) prompt-injection defenses that sanitize at the character level but not the token level. The closely-related second-place bug: case sensitivity. `"GitHub"`, `"github"`, `"Github"`, and `"GITHUB"` are entirely different token sequences, so a prompt that works in one casing can subtly underperform in another.

%%FILL: what a model with character-level tokenization would do better, and what it would do worse%%
**Better:** counting letters, spelling, character-level edits, novel-language transliteration, code with rare identifiers, robustness to typos and obfuscation, exact-match retrieval, regex reasoning, languages with rich morphology (Finnish, Turkish, Inuktitut), handling of arbitrary Unicode without byte-fallback shrapnel.

**Worse:** context window in semantic terms — a 200K char-token window holds ~50K English words vs ~150K for BPE, so long-document reasoning suffers ~3× compression loss. Training compute scales with sequence length quadratically (attention) — same FLOP budget yields a much weaker char-level model. Inference latency per "thought" is 3–5× higher because each semantic step requires 3–5× more tokens. The model also has to *learn* what BPE gives for free: that "ing" is a morpheme, that "the" is a function word, that "http://" is a unit. ByT5 and CANINE have shown this trade-off empirically — char-level wins on noisy/multilingual benchmarks, loses on long-context reasoning and pure throughput. The hybrid future is probably learned tokenization (Megabyte, MambaByte, BLT) where the model picks its own boundaries dynamically rather than committing to either extreme at training time.
