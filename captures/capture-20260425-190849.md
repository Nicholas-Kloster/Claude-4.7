%%FILL: the precise distinction between in-context learning and gradient-based fine-tuning%%

Fine-tuning mutates weights via backprop: gradients computed on a loss, weights updated, the change persists across all future inputs. ICL mutates nothing — weights are frozen. The "learning" is a forward-pass phenomenon: the prompt's tokens shift the residual stream such that the model's existing circuits produce outputs conditioned on the demonstrations. It's runtime conditioning, not parameter update. The distinction matters because it constrains what ICL can do — it can only reach behaviors expressible as a re-weighting of pre-existing capabilities the pretrained weights already encode. Fine-tuning can install a genuinely new capability; ICL can only elicit and recombine ones already latent.

%%FILL: induction heads as the mechanistic substrate — what they do and how their formation correlates with ICL emergence%%

Induction heads are a specific attention-head circuit (Olsson et al., Anthropic 2022) that implements pattern completion of the form `[A][B] ... [A] → [B]`. Mechanically it's two heads working in series: a "previous-token head" in an earlier layer copies token N-1's information into token N's residual stream, and a later head queries on the current token, attends back to the prior occurrence of the same token, and copies forward whatever followed it last time. The striking finding: the loss curve of small transformers shows a sharp phase change during training — a visible bump — and that bump aligns almost exactly with the moment induction heads form in the residual stream. ICL ability (measured as in-context loss decreasing with more examples) emerges at the same moment. The correlation is tight enough that induction heads are now treated as the load-bearing mechanism for at least the simplest forms of ICL — copying, pattern matching, few-shot label induction. More complex ICL (semantic generalization beyond surface patterns) likely involves additional circuitry built on top of this substrate, but the substrate itself is induction heads.

%%FILL: why ICL gets better with more examples up to a point and then plateaus or degrades%%

Three forces compete. (1) More examples narrow the implicit task posterior — each demonstration is evidence that disambiguates "which task am I doing." Gains here are largest in the first few shots and saturate fast, because most of the disambiguation work is done by example one or two. (2) Position-dependent attention dilution: as the prompt grows, attention mass spreads across more tokens, and the relevant demonstrations compete with irrelevant context for the head's attention budget. Past a threshold, signal-to-noise drops. (3) For long-context models specifically, lost-in-the-middle effects mean examples in the middle of a long prompt get attended to less than those at the ends, so adding middle examples can actively hurt. The plateau is where (1)'s diminishing returns meet (2)'s growing cost; the degradation regime is where (2) and (3) dominate. The shape of the curve is also task-dependent — tasks with high label entropy (many possible outputs) keep benefiting from examples longer than tasks with binary labels.

%%FILL: the order-of-examples sensitivity — why it exists and what it tells you about the mechanism%%

If ICL were truly Bayesian inference over a task distribution, order would not matter — the posterior is permutation-invariant in the examples. Empirically, order matters a lot: the same examples in different orders can swing accuracy by 10–30 points (Lu et al. 2022, "Fantastically Ordered Prompts"). Recency bias is part of it — the last example's label disproportionately influences predictions, consistent with attention falling off with distance. Majority-label bias is another — if the last few examples share a label, the model anchors there. This tells you ICL is not implementing clean Bayesian updating; it's running attention-based pattern matching where positional structure leaks into the inferred task. The mechanism is closer to "find the most recent analogous case and complete it" than "integrate evidence." It also tells you that prompt engineering's order-tuning effects aren't superstition — they're exploiting a real architectural bias.

%%FILL: what task vectors are and what their existence implies about how models represent tasks%%

Task vectors (Hendel et al. 2023, "In-Context Learning Creates Task Vectors") are a finding that the work a few-shot prompt does can be compressed into a single residual-stream vector at one specific layer. Procedure: run the model on a few-shot prompt, snapshot the residual stream at a chosen layer at the position right before the query, then on a fresh query (with no demonstrations) inject that vector at the same layer — and the model performs the task. The demonstrations themselves can be discarded; the vector alone carries the task. The implication is structural: tasks are represented as additive directions in activation space, and ICL's job is to read demonstrations and write the corresponding direction into the residual stream at a specific layer. This is much closer to "indexing into a learned dictionary of tasks" than "doing inference from scratch each forward pass." It also suggests the pretraining distribution implicitly carved up task-space into a basis the model can address compositionally — and that ICL is the addressing mechanism, not the storage.

%%FILL: why ICL works across modalities and tasks the model was never explicitly trained on%%

Because the substrate — pattern completion via induction-style circuitry — is modality-agnostic. The circuit operates on tokens (or patches, or codebook indices) and asks "given this prefix-suffix pattern, what comes next." It does not know or care that the tokens are English, code, protein sequence, image patches, or audio frames. Once the pretraining distribution is broad enough that the model has learned compositional features at multiple levels of abstraction, any new task expressible as a pattern over those features can be elicited. The "tasks the model was never explicitly trained on" framing is also slightly misleading — pretraining on web-scale data implicitly contains demonstrations of an enormous range of tasks (translation, summarization, format conversion, role-play, arithmetic, etc.) embedded in natural text. ICL on a "new" task is usually really ICL on a task the model saw fragments of during pretraining, now being assembled in a new combination. The genuinely novel-task case (e.g., a synthetic symbolic task with no natural-language analogue) works to the extent the task decomposes into primitives the model already has.

%%FILL: a specific claim about ICL that was widely believed in 2022 and has since been shown wrong%%

The claim: "ICL works because the model is doing implicit gradient descent — the forward pass on the few-shot prompt is mathematically equivalent to a gradient step on those examples." This came from von Oswald et al. (2022) and Akyürek et al. (2022), which showed that for linear-attention transformers on linear regression, you could construct weights such that the forward pass implements one step of gradient descent on the in-context examples. It was widely repeated as "ICL is gradient descent in disguise." Subsequent work has eroded this hard. The equivalence holds only for linear attention and linear tasks; real transformers use softmax attention, and the constructed weights are not what training actually finds. Empirical probes (Shen et al. 2024 and others) show real models' ICL behavior diverges from gradient-descent predictions on non-linear tasks, on tasks with structured label noise, and in how it responds to demonstration corruption. The current consensus is closer to: ICL is a heterogeneous bag of mechanisms — induction-head pattern matching, task-vector retrieval, Bayesian-ish posterior narrowing — and "implicit gradient descent" was a clean mathematical analogy that captured a special case and got over-generalized.
