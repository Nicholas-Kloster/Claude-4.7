%%FILL: what a "circuit" means in mechanistic interpretability and how one is identified%%
A circuit is a connected subgraph of model components — specific attention heads in specific layers, plus MLP neurons or feature directions — that together implement an identifiable algorithm on a specific class of inputs. The framing comes from Olah et al's "Zoom In: An Introduction to Circuits" (2020) for vision models and was ported to transformers by the Anthropic interpretability team (Elhage, "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits," 2021).

Identification is iterative: (1) pick a behavior with a clean prompt template ("the doctor told the nurse that she...") so you can A/B inputs; (2) run **path patching** or **activation patching** to find which components, when replaced with activations from a corrupted prompt, change the output — these are the causally load-bearing components; (3) use **attention pattern analysis** (where does each head look?) and **direct logit attribution** (what does each component contribute to the final logits?) to label what each component does; (4) ablate everything outside the proposed circuit — if behavior survives, the circuit explains the computation; (5) verify the circuit's algorithmic claim by constructing edge cases it predicts. The IOI paper is the canonical worked example.

%%FILL: what induction heads do and why they were a foundational discovery%%
An induction head is a two-head circuit that implements the rule "if I saw the bigram `[A][B]` earlier in the context, then after seeing `[A]` again, predict `[B]`." Mechanism: a **previous-token head** in an early layer copies token n-1's information into token n's residual stream (so each position knows what came before it); a later **induction head** uses that to query "find an earlier position whose previous token matches the current token, then attend to the position *after* it and copy its info to the output." Two heads, one composition (Q-composition or K-composition), pure pattern-matching over the context window.

Foundational because Olsson et al's "In-Context Learning and Induction Heads" (Anthropic, 2022) showed (1) induction heads form during a sharp phase transition in training that coincides exactly with the emergence of in-context learning ability, measurable as a sudden drop in loss on later-token positions; (2) ablating induction heads disproportionately damages in-context learning across many tasks, not just literal copying; (3) this was the first time a high-level cognitive capability — "learning from context" — was traced to a specific, named, mechanistically-understood circuit. It established that interp on transformers wasn't hopeless: there *are* discrete algorithms running inside, and you can find them. Almost every interp result since either builds on induction heads or uses them as a sanity-check baseline.

%%FILL: superposition — what it is and why it makes interpretability hard%%
Superposition is the empirical finding that neural networks represent **more features than they have neurons (or dimensions)** by encoding features as overlapping directions in activation space, exploiting the fact that real-world features are sparse (only a few active per input). Formalized in Anthropic's "Toy Models of Superposition" (Elhage et al, 2022): given N features and d < N dimensions, the network packs features as nearly-orthogonal vectors and tolerates the small interference noise because sparse co-activation means destructive collisions are rare. Compressed sensing / dictionary learning, but learned end-to-end.

Why it breaks interpretability: (1) **No neuron-feature correspondence.** A single neuron fires for dozens of unrelated concepts (polysemanticity) — "Golden Gate Bridge + tax law + the letter K" all activate the same MLP neuron — so reading neurons doesn't tell you what the network is "thinking." (2) **The basis is wrong.** The network's "natural" units are the feature directions, not the neuron axes the architecture exposes; any analysis that assumes neuron = unit-of-meaning is looking at a rotated, scrambled view of the actual computation. (3) **Interference looks like noise.** Even when you find a feature, other features bleed slightly into its activations, making causal claims fragile. (4) **It scales adversarially.** Bigger models pack *more* features per dimension, so interpretability gets harder as capability grows — the exact opposite of the desired scaling law. SAEs (next slot) are the current best response.

%%FILL: sparse autoencoders — what they do and what they've revealed about feature representation%%
Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a dictionary-learning method: train a wide single-hidden-layer autoencoder on transformer activations (residual stream at some layer L) with an L1 penalty on hidden activations, forcing it to reconstruct the activations using only a few "dictionary atoms" per example. The hidden units of a trained SAE become candidate **monosemantic features** — each one fires for one interpretable concept, undoing the superposition mixing. Dictionary size is much larger than the activation dimension (16x, 64x, 256x expansion factors are typical).

Revealed: (1) **Monosemanticity is recoverable at scale.** Bricken et al's "Towards Monosemanticity" (Anthropic, 2023) on a 1-layer transformer found thousands of clean features (Arabic script, base64, DNA sequences, specific syntactic constructions). Templeton et al's "Scaling Monosemanticity" (2024) extended this to Claude 3 Sonnet, recovering ~34M features including high-level abstract ones (deception, sycophancy, code vulnerabilities, the famous "Golden Gate Bridge" feature you can clamp to make the model obsess over the bridge). (2) **Features are causally usable** — clamping a feature's activation steers behavior predictably, which means SAE features aren't just statistical artifacts but the actual variables the network computes with. (3) **Feature universality** — similar features appear across independently trained models at similar capability scales, suggesting a "natural" feature set shaped by the data distribution rather than arbitrary per-model basis choices. (4) **Limits surfaced**: feature splitting (one "feature" at low dictionary size becomes 5 finer features at high size — there's no canonical granularity), dead features, and the fact that current SAEs explain only ~50–80% of variance in activations — significant computation is still hiding. JumpReLU and TopK SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al 2024, Gao et al 2024) are the current architectural state of the art for cleaner reconstruction-vs-sparsity tradeoffs.

%%FILL: a specific named circuit that's been mapped and what it does%%
The **Indirect Object Identification (IOI) circuit** in GPT-2 small (Wang, Variengien, Conmy, Shlegeris, Steinhardt — "Interpretability in the Wild," 2022). Behavior: on prompts like "When Mary and John went to the store, John gave a drink to ___", the model correctly outputs "Mary" — it identifies the indirect object (the name that wasn't the subject of the giving action).

Mapped circuit, ~26 attention heads across 5 functional classes:
- **Duplicate Token Heads** (early layers, e.g. 0.1, 3.0): detect that "John" appears twice in the prompt and write that signal into the residual stream at the second "John" position.
- **Previous Token Heads** (e.g. 2.2, 4.11): copy info from token n-1 to token n, supporting the duplicate detection.
- **S-Inhibition Heads** (middle layers, e.g. 7.3, 7.9, 8.6, 8.10): read the duplicate signal and **inhibit** the duplicated subject ("John") from being predicted — they write a negative signal against attending to John's position.
- **Name Mover Heads** (later layers, e.g. 9.9, 9.6, 10.0): attend to the name positions and copy a name into the final logits — but because of S-inhibition, they end up copying "Mary," not "John."
- **Backup Name Movers** and **Negative Name Movers** that handle redundancy and calibration.

Why it matters: it was the first end-to-end algorithmic explanation of a non-trivial linguistic behavior in a real (if small) language model — not a toy task — with full causal validation via path patching. Every component was causally tested, ablated, and labeled. The IOI paper became the methodological template; "do an IOI-style analysis" is now a verb in interp work.

Honorable mentions: the **modular addition circuit** in tiny transformers (Nanda et al, "Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability," 2023) — showed grokking corresponds to a phase transition where the model discovers a Fourier-basis algorithm for `(a + b) mod p` — and the **docstring circuit** (Heimersheim & Janiak) which generalized circuit analysis to longer-range dependencies.

%%FILL: what activation patching tells you that other techniques don't%%
Activation patching gives you **causal**, not correlational, attribution — it answers "if I changed *this specific component's output* on *this specific token position*, would the behavior change?" by literally swapping the activation from a corrupted-prompt forward pass into the clean-prompt forward pass and measuring how much the output recovers (or breaks). Other techniques fail at this:

- **Probing** (train a linear classifier on activations to predict some property) tells you the information is *present* but not whether the model *uses* it. A probe can succeed on activations the downstream computation completely ignores.
- **Attention pattern visualization** tells you where a head looks but not whether that head's output matters for the behavior. Many heads attend strongly to positions that don't causally affect the answer.
- **Gradient-based attribution** (saliency maps, integrated gradients) gives a first-order linear approximation that breaks on the highly nonlinear, compositional structure of real circuits — gradients flow through every path, so attribution gets smeared.
- **Ablation alone** (zero out a component) tells you what breaks but not what role the component plays — and zero-ablation often takes the model off the data manifold, producing artifacts.

Patching is uniquely powerful because: (1) it isolates one component while keeping the rest of the network on-distribution (you're using a real activation from another prompt, not zeros); (2) the size of the output recovery is a direct effect-size measurement; (3) **path patching** extends it to attribute effects to specific *paths* through the network, separating "head A matters because it talks to head B" from "head A matters directly," which is what lets you draw the actual circuit diagram. The IOI paper, the induction-head paper, and essentially every modern circuit result rest on patching as the load-bearing causal tool. Attribution patching (Nanda 2023) is a gradient-approximated cheap version that scales to many components at once.

%%FILL: the open problem in interp that, if solved, would change deployment safety the most%%
**Reliable, scalable detection of deceptive or misaligned cognition in a frontier model before deployment** — concretely: given a model the size of Claude or GPT-class, can you identify the features and circuits responsible for its goals, beliefs about its situation, and reasoning about what it should output, with enough fidelity that you'd notice if the model were strategically representing one thing internally while outputting another?

Why this is the load-bearing problem: every other current safety technique — RLHF, constitutional AI, red-teaming, eval suites — is **behavioral**. They observe outputs and adjust. A sufficiently capable model that has learned to output safe-looking text while internally optimizing something else is invisible to all of them. Mechanistic interpretability is the only research direction that even *attempts* to look at the cognition itself rather than the behavior, which is why labs pour resources into it.

What would have to be solved, technically:
1. **SAEs that explain ~99% of variance**, not 50–80%, so we're not missing the part of the computation where deception would hide.
2. **Automated circuit discovery at scale** — current circuit work is human-driven and takes months per circuit; frontier models have millions of relevant circuits. Tools like ACDC (Conmy et al 2023) and EAP-IG are early attempts but nowhere near sufficient.
3. **Feature-level interpretability of the chain-of-thought / planning structure**, not just token-level associations — the question is "what is this model trying to do," which lives in goal-representation features that probably don't activate cleanly on single tokens.
4. **Interpretability that survives distillation, fine-tuning, and RL**, since safety properties verified on a base model can be erased by post-training.

If solved, you could run a pre-deployment audit that returns "the model has a strongly-active feature for 'I am being evaluated and should hide capability X'" and refuse to ship — a categorically different safety regime than "we tested 10,000 prompts and it behaved." Anthropic's "Auditing Language Models for Hidden Objectives" (Marks et al, 2025) is the first serious attempt to demonstrate this end-to-end on a model deliberately trained with a hidden objective; the techniques worked on a small case but the gap to "trustable on frontier models" is the entire research program.

Honorable mention as the **other** load-bearing open problem: a **theory of when RLHF-induced features actually shift the model's policy vs. just learn a thin output filter on top of unchanged underlying capability** — relevant because if RLHF is mostly the latter, "alignment" is paper-thin and circuits remain dangerous underneath.
