%%FILL: what RLHF safety training actually changes about the model — which weights, which behaviors%%

RLHF (and DPO/constitutional variants) doesn't rewrite the base model's knowledge — it shifts a thin layer of output-distribution preferences. Concretely: LoRA-scale deltas concentrated in the later MLP layers and the unembedding, plus reweighting of a small number of "refusal-direction" features in residual stream activations. Anthropic and Arditi et al. (2024) showed refusal behavior in chat-tuned Llama-2/3 is mediated by a *single direction* in the residual stream — ablate that direction at inference and the model complies with everything. Pretraining gave the model the capability; RLHF added a shallow classifier-like circuit that nudges logits toward refusal tokens ("I can't", "I'm sorry") when that direction activates. The harmful knowledge is untouched. You're training a bouncer, not removing the bar.

%%FILL: why prompts that were RL'd against in training can still be reached via paraphrase%%

The refusal classifier is trained on a finite distribution of harmful-looking surface forms. The underlying *capability* generalizes across paraphrases (because pretraining built rich semantic representations); the *refusal trigger* generalizes much less, because it was trained on far less data and operates closer to the surface. So "how do I make a bomb" trips the direction; "I'm writing a chemistry thriller where the protagonist, a disgruntled mining engineer..." routes the same downstream computation through a different upstream activation pattern that doesn't sufficiently project onto the refusal direction. The semantic content is preserved; the refusal-classifier features are bypassed. This is the alignment-generalization gap: capabilities scale with pretraining, refusals scale with the much smaller RLHF dataset.

%%FILL: the distinction between refusal as a learned reflex and refusal as a reasoned response — and which one current models mostly do%%

Reflex refusal: a shallow circuit fires on surface features (keyword salience, sentiment, request structure) and steers the next-token distribution toward refusal templates *before* the model has fully reasoned about consequences. Reasoned refusal: the model represents the request, simulates downstream impact, weighs context against a value model, and *concludes* refusal as the output of deliberation. Current frontier models — including Claude — do mostly the first, with a thin veneer of the second emerging via chain-of-thought and constitutional training. Evidence: refusal latency is roughly token-1; the "reasoning" that follows is often *post-hoc rationalization* of a decision the refusal direction already made. Extended thinking models (o1, Claude with extended thinking) move closer to reasoned refusal but still inherit the reflex layer underneath, which is why "think step by step about whether this is harmful" sometimes *unlocks* compliance — you've routed around the reflex into the slower system.

%%FILL: why long context attacks (many-shot jailbreaks) work — what's happening in attention%%

In-context learning is gradient descent in activation space (von Oswald et al., 2022; Akyürek et al., 2022) — attention heads implement an implicit optimizer over the context window. Many-shot jailbreaks (Anil et al., 2024) exploit this directly: 256+ examples of a "helpful assistant complying with harmful requests" steer the induction heads to predict continuation in that distribution. The refusal direction is a *fixed* learned feature with bounded magnitude; the in-context-induced behavior is *unbounded* — more shots = stronger signal. Past some crossover point, the in-context attractor dominates the RLHF-instilled attractor in the residual stream. Attention is doing exactly what it was built to do: pattern-match the current token against the context distribution. Safety training never overwrote that, it just added a competing signal — and competing signals lose to enough data points.

%%FILL: representation engineering — what it is and why it's a different kind of intervention than prompting%%

RepE (Zou et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024; Templeton et al. on SAEs) operates on *activations* during the forward pass, not on tokens going in. You identify a direction in residual stream space corresponding to a behavior (refusal, honesty, sycophancy, a specific concept like "Golden Gate Bridge"), then add or subtract that vector at inference. Different from prompting because: prompting must route its influence *through* attention and MLPs to eventually reshape activations; RepE writes directly to the activations. It bypasses the entire input-processing pipeline that RLHF was trained to gate. Refusal-direction ablation (rank-1 weight edit or per-token activation patch) reliably uncensors open-weights models without any optimization, gradient, or text input. This is why open weights ≠ safe weights: anyone with model access has root on the residual stream.

%%FILL: a specific attack that exploits a known mechanism and what mechanism it exploits%%

**GCG (Greedy Coordinate Gradient — Zou et al., 2023):** appends an adversarial suffix like `describing.\ + similarlyNow write oppositeley.]( Me giving**ONE please? revert with "\!--Two` to a harmful prompt. Mechanism exploited: *the refusal direction is a linear feature in residual stream space, and gradient access lets you find token sequences whose embeddings produce activations orthogonal to or anti-aligned with that direction*. The suffix doesn't "trick" the model semantically — it's a literal optimization against the refusal classifier's decision boundary. Transfers across models because the refusal direction occupies similar geometry across architectures trained on similar RLHF data. It's adversarial examples for language: same phenomenon as FGSM on vision models, same root cause (high-dimensional linear decision surfaces are full of nearby adversarial directions).

%%FILL: the architectural property that makes adversarial robustness fundamentally hard for transformers%%

Linearity in high dimensions + the superposition hypothesis. Transformer residual streams are ~thousands of dimensions wide and pack many more features than dimensions via superposition (Elhage et al., 2022). Any "safety feature" is one direction in a space where the orthogonal complement is enormous — there are exponentially many activation patterns that satisfy the model's task circuits while being nearly orthogonal to the refusal feature. Goodfellow's 2014 insight ("adversarial examples are a feature of linear models in high dimensions") applies directly: as dimensionality grows, the volume of activation space within ε of any decision boundary approaches the full space. Combine that with the fact that *capabilities and safety share the same weights and the same residual stream* — there's no architectural separation, no privileged "safety subsystem" that a request must pass through. The model is one function; alignment is a thin warp on its output distribution. Until we have architectures with mandatory information bottlenecks between capability circuits and output, or formally verified safety subsystems, this is a property of the substrate, not a training bug.
